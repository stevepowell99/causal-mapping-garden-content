"Sociology... is a science which attempts the interpretive understanding of social action in order thereby to arrive at a causal explanation of its course and effects."

- Weber, M. (1978). Economy and Society: An Outline of Interpretive Sociology. (G. Roth & C. Wittich, Eds.). University of California Press. (Original work published 1921).


Weber's concept of _Verstehen_ (interpretive understanding) was explicitly about grasping the causal significance of meaning — not just what happened, but _why actors thought it made sense_ to do what they did.

**Karl Marx** — on the constrained causality of human action:

"Men make their own history, but they do not make it as they please; they do not make it under self-selected circumstances, but under circumstances existing already, given and transmitted from the past. The tradition of all dead generations weighs like a nightmare on the brains of the living." [Marxists.org](https://www.marxists.org/archive/marx/works/1852/18th-brumaire/ch01.htm)

— Marx, K. (1852). _The Eighteenth Brumaire of Louis Bonaparte_, ch. 1.

This is arguably the founding statement of structure-agency causation — people _cause_ things to happen, but only within conditions they didn't choose. Causes are real but always contextual and historically embedded.

And from the Preface to _A Contribution to the Critique of Political Economy_ (1859), the classic formulation of social determination:

"It is not the consciousness of men that determines their being, but, on the contrary, their social being that determines their consciousness."

— Marx, K. (1859). _A Contribution to the Critique of Political Economy_, Preface.

**Hannah Arendt** — on the boundlessness and unpredictability of social causation:

"Action, though it may proceed from nowhere, so to speak, acts into a medium where every reaction becomes a chain reaction and where every process is the cause of new processes." 

— Arendt, H. (1958). _The Human Condition_. University of Chicago Press.

And relatedly:

"The reason why we are never able to foretell with certainty the outcome and end of any action is simply that action has no end." [Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy](https://plato.stanford.edu/archives/fall2016/entries/arendt/)

— Arendt, H. (1958). _The Human Condition_. p. 233.

Arendt is making a deep point about why causal explanation in human affairs can never be fully predictive — because action enters into a _web of relationships_ where causation ramifies without limit. She also argued that the "new always happens against the overwhelming odds of statistical laws and their probability" [Internet Encyclopedia of Philosophy](https://iep.utm.edu/hannah-arendt/) — a direct challenge to probabilistic causal models.

**Kurt Lewin** — on behaviour as a function of person and environment:
"Every psychological event depends upon the state of the person and at the same time on the environment, although their relative importance is different in different cases."

— Lewin, K. (1936). _Principles of Topological Psychology_. McGraw-Hill. p. 24.

His famous formula **B = f(P, E)** — behaviour is a function of the person and their environment — is essentially a causal claim that refuses to separate individual from context.

And on the relationship between understanding and intervention:

"If you want truly to understand something, try to change it." [A-Z Quotes](https://www.azquotes.com/author/21978-Kurt_Lewin)

— Widely attributed to Lewin (though the precise sourcing is disputed; see Tolman, 1996).

And of course:

"The research needed for social practice can best be characterized as research for social management or social engineering. It is a type of action research, a comparative research of the conditions and effects of various forms of social action, and research leading to social action. Research that produces nothing but books will not suffice." [Wikiquote](https://en.wikiquote.org/wiki/Kurt_Lewin)

— Lewin, K. (1946). Action research and minority problems. _Journal of Social Issues_, 2(4), 34–46. (Reproduced in Lewin, 1948, p. 202–3.)



**Sigmund Freud** — on psychic causation and the rejection of randomness in mental life:

"Nothing in the mind is arbitrary or undetermined."

This is Freud's principle of _psychic determinism_, articulated most fully in:

— Freud, S. (1901). _The Psychopathology of Everyday Life_ (_Zur Psychopathologie des Alltagslebens_). (Standard Edition, Vol. 6.)

And from _The Interpretation of Dreams_:

"The dream is not meaningless, not absurd... it is a completely valid psychical phenomenon, actually a wish-fulfilment; it may be enrolled in the chain of intelligible psychical acts of the waking state; it has been built up by a highly complicated intellectual activity."

— Freud, S. (1900). _The Interpretation of Dreams_. (Standard Edition, Vols. 4–5.) ch. 3.

Freud's whole project was essentially a causal one: every symptom, slip, and dream has causes, but those causes are _meanings_ — intentions, wishes, conflicts — not mechanical forces. A qualitative causal model if ever there was one.

**Jürgen Habermas** — on the relationship between causal explanation and understanding:

Habermas argued in _Knowledge and Human Interests_ (1968) that the social sciences require a form of knowledge that integrates causal explanation with interpretive understanding, and that emancipatory knowledge involves recognising the _causal power of ideology_ — how systematically distorted communication causes people to misrecognise their own interests.

"The self-reflection of a science… requires a going beyond this science into the reflection on the conditions of the possibility of knowledge and action."

— Habermas, J. (1968). _Knowledge and Human Interests_ (_Erkenntnis und Interesse_). (Trans. J. Shapiro, 1971). Beacon Press.

He explicitly argued that the positivist separation of facts from values, and of causal explanation from interpretive understanding, was itself ideological.

— Miles, M. B. & Huberman, A. M. (1994). _Qualitative Data Analysis: An Expanded Sourcebook_ (2nd ed.). Sage. p. 147.

**On realist/generative causation**

"The task of science is the production of the knowledge of those enduring and continually active mechanisms of nature that produce the phenomena of the world." [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roy_Bhaskar)

— Bhaskar, R. (1975). _A Realist Theory of Science_. Leeds Books.

---

"Causal outcomes follow from mechanisms acting in contexts." [ResearchGate](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/248326364_Realistic_EvaluationRay_Pawson_and_Nick_Tilley_Sage_London_1997_256_pages)

— Pawson, R. & Tilley, N. (1997). _Realistic Evaluation_. Sage. (ch. 3)

**On the modus operandi approach**

"Each cause has a set of footprints, a short one if it's a proximate cause, a long one if it's a remote cause, but in general the modus operandi is a sequence of intermediate or concurrent events or a set of conditions, or a chain of events, that has to be present when the cause is effective." [Better Evaluation](https://www.betterevaluation.org/methods-approaches/methods/modus-operandi)

— Scriven, M. (2008). A summative evaluation of RCT methodology: An alternative approach to causal research. _Journal of Multidisciplinary Evaluation_, 5(9), 11–24.

**On reasons as causes**

"People's motives or reasons for undertaking certain behaviors could not form part of general laws governing those behaviors, but … those same motives or reasons could, in the individual case, cause the behaviors to be carried out." 

— Mohr, L. B. (1996). _The Causes of Human Behavior: Implications for Theory and Method in the Social Sciences_. University of Michigan Press. p. vi.

---

"Agents are defined in terms of their tendencies and powers, among which in the case of human agents, are their reasons for acting." [ResearchGate](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/229713728_Exploring_the_Concept_of_Causal_Power_in_a_Critical_Realist_Tradition)

— Bhaskar, R. (1979). _The Possibility of Naturalism_. Harvester Press. p. 118.

**On qualitative/idiographic causal reasoning**

"Idiographic causal explanations are bound within people's stories and interpretations. Usually, these are expressed through words." [Pressbooks](https://uta.pressbooks.pub/foundationsofsocialworkresearch/chapter/4-2-causality/)

— From _Foundations of Social Work Research_ (open textbook), ch. 4.2 on Causality.

---

"Realism replaces the regularity model with one in which objects and social relations have causal powers which may or may not produce regularities, and which can be explained independently of them." [Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10833-021-09415-5)

— Sayer, A. (1992). _Method in Social Science: A Realist Approach_ (2nd ed.). Routledge. pp. 2–3.

**On evaluation and the "what works" question**

The central realist evaluation question is not simply "does this intervention work?" but rather "what works, for whom, in what respects, to what extent, in what contexts, and how?" [Better Evaluation](https://www.betterevaluation.org/methods-approaches/approaches/realist-evaluation)

— Pawson, R. & Tilley, N. (1997). _Realistic Evaluation_. Sage.

**On the fundamental task of social science**

"The explanation of social phenomena by revealing the causal mechanisms which produce them is the fundamental task of research." [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1080/13645579.2023.2173845)

— Danermark, B., Ekström, M., Jakobsen, L. & Karlsson, J. C. (2002). _Explaining Society: Critical Realism in the Social Sciences_. Routledge. p. 1.
