## But indeed we code opposites and hierarchies


Useful "tweaks" exist that allow the factor labels to perform much of the analytical work. An example is **hierarchical coding**, which groups cause and effect codes. This structure is displayed directly within the labels, such as "Health behaviour; hand washing," where the semicolon can be read as "in particular". These devices, combined with associated inference rules, are claimed to cover approximately 90% of causal claims in most texts.



## Opposites
For instance, if one person says, "Drugs make you happy," and another person says, "Drugs make you unhappy," we would code that with two different labels, such as "being happy" and "being unhappy" . We do this without necessarily assuming, out of the box, that these are opposite points on some kind of latent variable . It is surprising how much of the time this very minimalist approach is quite adequate to capture most of the causal claims that people make .

## Limitations of Minimalism: Enablers and Blockers

However, we are under no illusions that this approach is exhaustive . There are plenty of causal claims, and an unending number of different possible causal claims, that people could make which cannot be captured with this minimalist approach . Even though these claims are relatively rare, it would still be nice to be able to capture them .

The Enabler Case

In the case of **enablers**, one can do quite well with a minimalist approach .

For example, consider the statement: "I went on holiday to Spain expecting to enjoy it, and indeed, I did particularly enjoy it because I remembered to take my phrase book" . One could argue whether the phrase book in this case is a purely Boolean enabler or some kind of amplifier . Under minimalist coding, this scenario would result in two claims : (1) "I went on holiday" influenced "I enjoyed it," and (2) "I took my phrase book" also influenced "My enjoyment of the holiday" .

The Blocker Case

Minimalist coding works less well when dealing with **blockers** .

For example: "I went on holiday to Spain, but I forgot to take my phrase book so I didn't enjoy the holiday at all" . You can certainly code it as, "I forgot to take my phrase book, therefore I didn't enjoy the holiday" . However, our intuition that "Going on holiday" should also be part of the equation somehow breaks the model [vii, viii]. There seems to be no reason at all to code anything of the form "I went on holiday" with any kind of causal arrow to "I didn't enjoy the holiday" [viii].

This insight reflects back on the enabler case, where it wasn't really adequate to simply have two undifferentiated causal links -- one from the holiday and one from the phrase book -- to the enjoyment of the holiday [viii].

Moving Towards a Realist Understanding

From a positivist or frequentist perspective, one might suggest that all the other values of the truth tables are somewhere in the background, implying that blocker and enabler claims are making a total of four claims about what happens across different combinations of truth values [ix]. However, this is a long way from the position we take, and it seems absurd to suggest that those actual claims are somehow hidden in the main claim, even if they are arguably implied [ix].

Therefore, it seems that truly minimalist causation cannot fully capture these scenarios [x].

If one adopts a philosophical **realist understanding of causation** (as opposed to a realist evaluation point of view) [x], then what we code is that a cause "acts causally influenced, by virtue of its causal power to do so" [x].

Coding Enablers Using Realist Causation

Using this realist approach, we code the enabler case with two distinct elements [x, xi]:

1. **One arrow from "Going on holiday" to "enjoying the holiday"** [x].

2. **A second arrow from "Remembering to take the phrase book"** to that first causal link (the mechanism, including the two nodes and the arrow between them) [xi].

This second arrow is conventionally drawn to the middle of the first arrow [xi]. This structure is interpreted as: **"The phrase book enabled the causal link from X to Y, in virtue of its causal power to do so"** [xi].

Coding Blockers Using Realist Causation

The blocker case is visually almost the same [xi], but it requires adding a second concept to the underlying metaphysics [xii]: **the concept of the causal power to stop something** [xii].

### Limitations and Difficult Cases

There are kinds of causal claims that are difficult to cover with this minimalist coding.

1. **Causal Packages/Conjunctions:** Claims stating that a combination of factors (e.g., A and B) is needed to achieve C, such as "an accelerant and a spark to set fire," are problematic. Coding them separately ("accelerant causes fire" and "spark causes fire") misses the point. Coding the cause as a simple phrase ("an accelerant and spark") works but prevents the phrase from being parsed usefully (e.g., breaking it down to relate to claims just about sparks). While special symbols could link conjunctions, extending this approach to handle multiplication or non-linear associations is unclear. (For multiple independent causal claims, such as A causes C and G causes F, the convention is to record them separately, analogous to linear superposition).
    
2. **Blockers and Enablers:** These are flummoxing cases. A blocker (e.g., "My lack of a phrase book caused the normal causal power of a Spanish holiday to make me happy to be blocked") suggests a causal claim _about_ a causal relationship. While the claim can be written down, breaking it into constituent parts to apply rules is difficult. We do not fundamentally distinguish between absences and presences in the text (e.g., "rain dripping" versus "rain stopping dripping"). However, enablers and blockers appear **asymmetrical**. An enabler (e.g., presence of phrase book enabling the holiday to cause happiness) can be primitively coded as independent causes (phrase book caused happiness and holiday caused happiness), but the blocker case (e.g., lack of phrase book causing lack of happiness) is not as clear, as the holiday factor’s role is ambiguous. Treating them by invoking intermediate variables also confirms the asymmetry, as the blocking factor destroys the causal link, making the original cause (the holiday) seem to be doing nothing.
